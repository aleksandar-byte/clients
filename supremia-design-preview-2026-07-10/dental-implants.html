Who implants can help

Start with the problem you are trying to solve.

Implants are not one single treatment. The right plan depends on how many teeth are missing, what condition the surrounding teeth are in, how much bone is available, and whether your gums and health can support healing.

01

I lost one tooth and want it to feel normal again.

A single implant-supported crown may replace the missing tooth without reshaping healthy neighboring teeth for a bridge.

02

Several teeth are missing or failing.

Implants may support a bridge or staged restoration, depending on bone, spacing, bite forces, and the long-term condition of nearby teeth.

03

My denture moves when I chew or talk.

Implant-supported dentures can add stability and confidence compared with removable dentures that only rest on the gums.

What an implant does

The implant replaces the root. The restoration replaces the tooth.

A dental implant is a small post placed in the jawbone to support a custom tooth, bridge, or denture. Patients often say “implant” when they mean the whole finished result, but the plan usually includes three parts.

Restoration Crown, bridge, or denture

The visible part designed for chewing, speech, and smile appearance.

Connector Abutment

The small piece that connects the implant post to the final restoration.

Foundation Implant post

The artificial tooth root placed in bone after careful planning.

How planning works

A good implant plan answers the hard questions early.

Some implant cases are straightforward. Others need gum therapy, bone grafting, extraction planning, bite changes, sedation, or a phased smile plan. The consultation is where those pieces get sorted before anyone rushes treatment.

  1. Exam and imaging

    Review the missing tooth area, gums, bite, health history, and digital imaging.

  2. Readiness check

    Confirm whether bone support, gum health, and medical factors are favorable.

  3. Placement and healing

    Place the implant with appropriate comfort support, then allow time for healing.

  4. Final restoration

    Attach the custom crown, bridge, or denture once the foundation is ready.

Implant options

The right version depends on the gap, not the name of the procedure.

Single implant crown

Often considered when one tooth is missing and the neighboring teeth are healthy.

Implant-supported bridge

May replace several teeth in a row without relying only on removable appliances.

Implant-supported denture

Can help patients who want more denture stability for chewing and speaking.

Full-arch implant planning

Sometimes called All-on-4 style treatment, this is for more extensive tooth loss.

Dental implant FAQ

Questions patients usually ask before they schedule.

Can dental implants replace more than one tooth?

Yes. Depending on the case, implants can support a single crown, a bridge, a denture, or a full-arch restoration.

Do I need enough bone for an implant?

Bone support matters. If there is not enough support, bone grafting or another treatment sequence may be discussed before implant placement.

How long does implant treatment take?

Timing varies. Some steps can be efficient, but healing between placement and restoration can take months depending on the patient and the plan.

Are implants better than dentures or bridges?

Not always for every patient. Implants can offer strong support and avoid reshaping some neighboring teeth, but health, bone, cost, timing, and personal preference all matter.

What is the first step?

Start with an implant consultation so the team can evaluate the missing tooth area, take imaging, discuss comfort options, and compare realistic treatment paths.
